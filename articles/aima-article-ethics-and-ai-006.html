AI Ethics

The Algorithm
and the Soul

JS
Joselito Sering
Editor-in-Chief · AIMA
Published
April 28, 2026
Read Time
14 min read
Category
AI Ethics

There is no neutral position on artificial intelligence. Every company that deploys it makes a moral choice — whether to use it to reduce costs at the expense of workers, or to amplify human capacity in ways that make life more dignified, more safe, and more humane. The technology itself does not decide. We do. And right now, western society is doing a poor job of deciding together, which is why the conversation — urgent, messy, and morally loaded — has never mattered more.

The Fear Is Real

Let's begin with honesty: the anxiety surrounding AI and jobs is not hysteria. It is a rational response to observable facts. In 2025 alone, 342 tech company layoffs eliminated nearly 78,000 positions, with AI automation cited as a primary driver. That is approximately 491 people losing their livelihoods every single day. IBM replaced 8,000 HR department workers with AI agents. Microsoft, while revealing that 30% of its codebase is now AI-written, simultaneously cut over 6,000 jobs — with software engineers bearing the brunt of those cuts.

These are not projections. These are quarterly earnings disclosures and official company communications. And they arrive alongside forecasts that amplify the dread: the World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report found that 41% of global employers intend to reduce their workforce within five years due to AI automation. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei — who builds the very systems in question — has publicly stated that AI could eliminate roughly half of all entry-level white-collar positions within five years.

⚠️
Disproportionate Impact
The burden of AI displacement is not shared equally. Research cited by Cornell University and Revelio Labs shows that approximately 79% of employed women in the United States hold positions at high risk of automation, compared to 58% of men. Entry-level jobs — disproportionately held by young workers and people of color — are contracting fastest. Workers aged 18–24 are 129% more likely than workers over 65 to fear AI will make their jobs obsolete. This is not a universal disruption. It is a targeted one.

The psychological toll extends beyond job loss itself. Workers who remain in AI-exposed roles report elevated anxiety, reduced sense of purpose, and a creeping feeling of replaceability — even when their positions have not yet been eliminated. The fear of the algorithm is becoming its own mental health crisis, distinct from, but intertwined with, the economic one.

Profit Over People

Not all companies deploy AI with the same intent. Some are quite transparent that cost reduction — specifically, the reduction of labor costs — is the primary driver. This is not inherently wrong; productivity gains have historically benefited society over long time horizons. But the manner and speed of displacement matters enormously, as does who absorbs the losses in the short term.

The economic logic is seductive. AI chatbots reduce telemarketing costs by roughly 80%. Customer service automation that would have required thousands of human agents can now be handled by a small team managing AI systems. Goldman Sachs estimates that AI could replace the equivalent of 300 million full-time jobs globally, while potentially increasing global GDP by 7% annually over the decade following widespread adoption. Shareholders benefit immediately. Workers — particularly those without the resources or time to retrain — absorb the cost over years.

When a company eliminates 8,000 jobs for efficiency, the productivity gains compound in the boardroom. The pain compounds in communities.

— AIMA Editorial, April 2026

The pattern repeats across sectors. In healthcare, Kaiser Permanente replaced licensed clinical triage professionals with unlicensed operators following AI-generated scripts, triggering a 24-hour strike by 2,400 mental health workers in Northern California in 2025. In financial services, AI reads thousands of reports in minutes, compressing analyst roles. In creative industries — the very space AIMA operates in — content generation tools are being used by some companies not to augment human creatives, but to eliminate them entirely, producing "good enough" copy at pennies per word.

The Data Asymmetry

One underappreciated dimension of the profit-driven AI rollout is the data asymmetry it creates. Companies train large language models and predictive systems on human-generated content, labor, and knowledge — often without compensation to the humans who created that training data. There is an ethical debt embedded in every model weight that few companies are being asked to repay.

AI for Social Good

The same technology that displaces workers can, in the right hands, genuinely extend human welfare. This is not wishful thinking — it is already happening in measurable ways. The critical question is scale and intention.

In mental health, the shortage of licensed professionals in the United States is acute. AI-powered platforms are beginning to close that gap — not by replacing therapists, but by dramatically extending their reach. Companies like Talkspace, Unmind, and Mindstrong are deploying AI to match patients with providers, handle documentation tasks that consume hours of clinician time, and provide 24/7 support between sessions. Unmind reports that 71% of employees who used its platform felt less stressed, and 62% reported reduced anxiety.

✓
AI as Clinician's Ally
Ochsner Health implemented an AI-based scheduling system for anesthesiologists. Within six months, the time required to generate department schedules dropped from 60–75 hours per month to just 14 hours — freeing hundreds of hours annually for patient care. The AI did not replace a single physician. It gave physicians their time back.

In education, AI tutoring systems are providing personalized learning pathways to students in under-resourced schools that could never afford one-on-one instruction. In climate science, AI is accelerating the modeling of energy systems, agricultural yields, and ocean temperature that would take human scientists decades to compute. In disaster response, computer vision systems are analyzing satellite imagery to identify structural damage within hours of earthquakes or floods, directing aid to where it is most needed.

The Hard Facts

The honest data is more nuanced than either the doomsayers or the techno-optimists prefer. Goldman Sachs Research estimates that approximately 2.5% of US employment would be at risk of direct displacement under current AI adoption trajectories — significant, but not civilizational collapse. Their more expansive projection puts it at 6–7% if AI is widely adopted across all sectors, with new jobs expected to absorb much of the impact over time.

The World Economic Forum projects that by 2030, approximately 92 million jobs will be displaced by AI and automation — while 170 million new ones will emerge. But these are not one-to-one swaps. The new jobs do not appear in the same locations, in the same industries, or for the same people whose jobs disappeared.

💡
The Most Vulnerable Workers
The National Bureau of Economic Research's 2025 occupational analysis found that approximately 3.9% of US workers — 5 to 6 million people — sit at the intersection of high AI exposure and low adaptive capacity. These are workers in routine roles, with limited savings, in labor markets with few alternative opportunities. This is where the genuine hardship concentrates.

What has arrived is a structural shift in who gets hired into entry-level roles. Entry-level job postings overall have declined approximately 35% since January 2023, according to Revelio Labs data. The pipeline is narrowing at exactly the moment when the next generation most needs to enter it.

What Future Do We Want?

Here is where we must stop counting and start thinking philosophically. Because the statistics tell us what is happening. They do not tell us what should happen. That is a human question — and one that AI, no matter how sophisticated, cannot answer for us.

We stand at a genuinely unprecedented inflection point. Throughout human history, technological revolutions have displaced certain types of labor while creating new categories of work. The current wave is measured in quarters. A worker whose role is automated this year has not a decade but perhaps eighteen months to acquire new skills before the next wave makes even those skills insufficient.

The question is not whether machines can do your job. The question is whether we have built a society where a machine doing your job still provides you a life worth living.

— AIMA Editorial, April 2026

And then there is the question of Artificial General Intelligence. AGI — a system capable of performing any intellectual task a human can perform, across any domain — does not yet exist. But the world's leading AI laboratories are openly pursuing it. Leading AI scientists including Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio have warned that a poorly aligned AGI could pose existential risks comparable to nuclear war or pandemic. Hinton won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2024 for his foundational work in neural networks. When he says he is frightened, it is worth paying attention.

What kind of world do we want to inhabit alongside increasingly powerful AI systems? A world where the gains of automation flow primarily to capital, concentrating wealth further in an already unequal society? Or a world where productivity gains fund universal education, healthcare, and a basic income that acknowledges the changed nature of work? These are not technical questions. They are political, moral, and spiritual ones.

Building Guardrails

The good news is that the world is not passively watching. Regulatory frameworks are emerging, imperfectly but meaningfully, to constrain the worst applications of AI and create accountability structures for the rest.

The European Union's AI Act is the most comprehensive regulatory framework currently in force. It adopts a risk-based approach: AI systems are classified as unacceptable risk (banned outright), high risk (strict requirements), limited risk (transparency obligations), or minimal risk (largely unregulated). The prohibition on AI systems using manipulative or deceptive techniques took effect in February 2025. Rules governing general-purpose AI models became enforceable in August 2025.

🏛️
The EU AI Act Risk Tiers
Unacceptable risk: Real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring systems — banned. High risk: AI in critical infrastructure, education, employment, law enforcement — strict transparency and human oversight required. Limited risk: Chatbots — must disclose AI nature. Minimal risk: Spam filters — largely unregulated.

What Companies Can Do Now

Commit to human-in-the-loop requirements for high-stakes decisions: hiring, firing, credit, healthcare, and criminal justice should never be determined by an algorithm alone. Establish internal AI ethics boards with genuine authority. Conduct algorithmic audits for bias before deployment. Share the productivity gains: if AI doubles your team's output, invest meaningfully in the people who made that possible.

What Policymakers Can Do Now

Fund retraining programs at the scale the problem demands. The United States currently spends less than 0.1% of GDP on active labor market policies — one of the lowest rates in the developed world, and woefully insufficient for the pace of change underway. Establish liability frameworks: when an AI system causes harm, there must be a clear legal answer to the question "who is responsible?"

What Individuals Can Do Now

Learn to work alongside AI systems rather than in opposition to them — not because the technology is inevitably right, but because fluency provides agency. And maintain genuine human skills that AI cannot replicate: empathy, moral reasoning, contextual judgment, relational trust. These are not soft skills. They are the core of what makes human beings irreplaceable in each other's lives.

The Unplug Question

Here is a thought experiment worth sitting with. AI cannot function without electricity. Every inference, every model training run, every chatbot response depends on an unbroken chain of power generation, transmission infrastructure, data center cooling, and hardware manufacturing. The AI boom is already straining electrical grids; data centers are projected to consume 8% of US electricity by 2030.

Now consider the inverse question: can humans exist without electricity? Technically, yes — for most of human history, we did. But modern western civilization — its food supply chains, water treatment, medical infrastructure, communication networks, financial systems — is now so thoroughly dependent on continuous electrical power that a sustained grid failure at scale would be catastrophic. We have built a civilization that cannot function without the same resource AI requires to operate.

This shared dependency is not a warning against technology. It is a reminder that we are not choosing between a technological future and a non-technological one. We are choosing what kind of technological future to build. The electricity flows either way. What changes is who benefits from the systems it powers.

The most urgent AI governance question is therefore not "should we have AI?" — that question is already answered by economic and competitive reality. The question is: on whose terms, toward whose ends, under whose oversight, and with what protections for those who bear the costs?

🌐
The Choice Ahead
AI can diagnose disease earlier, extend mental health access to the underserved, accelerate climate solutions, educate children who have no teachers, and free workers from the most dehumanizing forms of labor. It can also concentrate wealth, surveil populations, and eliminate livelihoods without replacement. The technology does not determine which of these futures arrives. We do.

Glossary

Artificial Intelligence (AI) ↑ Back to text

The simulation of human cognitive functions — reasoning, learning, problem-solving, pattern recognition — by computer systems. Modern AI ranges from narrow, task-specific systems to large foundation models capable of generalized language and reasoning tasks across many domains.

AI Agents ↑ Back to text

Autonomous AI systems that can perceive their environment, make decisions, and take sequences of actions to achieve defined goals — without requiring a human to direct each individual step. In enterprise contexts, AI agents are increasingly used to handle workflows that once required human employees.

Large Language Model (LLM) ↑ Back to text

A type of AI system trained on massive datasets of text to predict and generate human-like language. LLMs such as GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini can write, summarize, translate, code, and reason across a wide range of tasks. They are the foundation of most current generative AI products.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) ↑ Back to text

A hypothetical AI system capable of performing any intellectual task that a human being can perform, with comparable flexibility and generality — not just within a specific domain. AGI does not currently exist. Its development timelines are contested among researchers, ranging from within a decade to potentially never.

General-Purpose AI (GPAI) ↑ Back to text

Under the EU AI Act, general-purpose AI refers to AI models — including large language models — that can perform a wide range of tasks and serve as the foundation for many downstream applications. GPAI models became subject to specific EU regulatory requirements in August 2025.

Computer Vision ↑ Back to text

A branch of AI concerned with enabling machines to interpret and understand visual information from the world — images, video, satellite data. Applications include medical imaging diagnostics, autonomous vehicle perception, satellite damage assessment for disaster response, and industrial quality control.

Algorithmic Bias

Systematic and repeatable errors or unfair outcomes in an AI system's outputs that reflect and often amplify biases present in training data, system design, or the way outputs are applied. Algorithmic bias has been documented in hiring tools, facial recognition systems, credit scoring, and predictive policing.

Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)

A design principle for AI systems that requires meaningful human review or intervention at critical decision points — particularly for high-stakes outcomes such as hiring, medical diagnosis, loan approval, or criminal justice. HITL requirements are mandated for high-risk AI applications under the EU AI Act.
