AI Ethics

The Algorithm
and the Soul

AI Ethics

There is no neutral position on artificial intelligence. Every company that deploys it makes a moral choice — whether to use it to reduce costs at the expense of workers, or to amplify human capacity in ways that make life more dignified, more safe, and more humane. The technology itself does not decide. We do. And right now, western society is doing a poor job of deciding together, which is why the conversation — urgent, messy, and morally loaded — has never mattered more.

The Fear Is Real

Let's begin with honesty: the anxiety surrounding AI and jobs is not hysteria. It is a rational response to observable facts. In 2025 alone, 342 tech company layoffs eliminated nearly 78,000 positions, with AI automation cited as a primary driver. That is approximately 491 people losing their livelihoods every single day. IBM replaced 8,000 HR department workers with AI agents. Microsoft, while revealing that 30% of its codebase is now AI-written, simultaneously cut over 6,000 jobs — with software engineers bearing the brunt of those cuts.

These are not projections. These are quarterly earnings disclosures and official company communications. And they arrive alongside forecasts that amplify the dread: the World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report found that 41% of global employers intend to reduce their workforce within five years due to AI automation. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei — who builds the very systems in question — has publicly stated that AI could eliminate roughly half of all entry-level white-collar positions within five years.

⚠️
Disproportionate Impact
The burden of AI displacement is not shared equally. Research cited by Cornell University and Revelio Labs shows that approximately 79% of employed women in the United States hold positions at high risk of automation, compared to 58% of men. Entry-level jobs — disproportionately held by young workers and people of color — are contracting fastest. Workers aged 18–24 are 129% more likely than workers over 65 to fear AI will make their jobs obsolete. This is not a universal disruption. It is a targeted one.

The psychological toll extends beyond job loss itself. Workers who remain in AI-exposed roles report elevated anxiety, reduced sense of purpose, and a creeping feeling of replaceability — even when their positions have not yet been eliminated. The fear of the algorithm is becoming its own mental health crisis, distinct from, but intertwined with, the economic one.

Profit Over People

Not all companies deploy AI with the same intent. Some are quite transparent that cost reduction — specifically, the reduction of labor costs — is the primary driver. This is not inherently wrong; productivity gains have historically benefited society over long time horizons. But the manner and speed of displacement matters enormously, as does who absorbs the losses in the short term.

The economic logic is seductive. AI chatbots reduce telemarketing costs by roughly 80%. Customer service automation that would have required thousands of human agents can now be handled by a small team managing AI systems. Goldman Sachs estimates that AI could replace the equivalent of 300 million full-time jobs globally, while potentially increasing global GDP by 7% annually over the decade following widespread adoption. Shareholders benefit immediately. Workers — particularly those without the resources or time to retrain — absorb the cost over years.

When a company eliminates 8,000 jobs for efficiency, the productivity gains compound in the boardroom. The pain compounds in communities.

— AIMA Editorial, April 2026

The pattern repeats across sectors. In healthcare, Kaiser Permanente replaced licensed clinical triage professionals with unlicensed operators following AI-generated scripts, triggering a 24-hour strike by 2,400 mental health workers in Northern California in 2025. In financial services, AI reads thousands of reports in minutes, compressing analyst roles. In creative industries — the very space AIMA operates in — content generation tools are being used by some companies not to augment human creatives, but to eliminate them entirely, producing "good enough" copy at pennies per word.

The distinction that matters is motive. Deploying AI to free humans from drudge work so they can do more meaningful and higher-value tasks is a legitimate, even admirable goal. Deploying AI primarily to extract the same or greater output while cutting payroll is a choice to transfer wealth upward. Both use the same technology. Only one serves society.

The Data Asymmetry

One underappreciated dimension of the profit-driven AI rollout is the data asymmetry it creates. Companies train large language models and predictive systems on human-generated content, labor, and knowledge — often without compensation to the humans who created that training data. The AI that replaces a customer service worker was built, in part, by analyzing millions of customer service interactions conducted by human workers. The model that summarizes legal documents was trained on legal briefs written by attorneys. There is an ethical debt embedded in every model weight that few companies are being asked to repay.

AI for Social Good

The same technology that displaces workers can, in the right hands, genuinely extend human welfare. This is not wishful thinking — it is already happening in measurable ways. The critical question is scale and intention.

In mental health, the shortage of licensed professionals in the United States is acute. Access to care is limited by cost, geography, and waitlists that stretch for months. AI-powered platforms are beginning to close that gap — not by replacing therapists, but by dramatically extending their reach. Companies like Talkspace, Unmind, and Mindstrong are deploying AI to match patients with providers, handle documentation tasks that consume hours of clinician time, and provide 24/7 support between sessions. Unmind reports that 71% of employees who used its platform felt less stressed, and 62% reported reduced anxiety. The American Psychological Association, as of 2025, has not seen mental health jobs replaced by AI — rather, documentation and billing automation is freeing clinicians to spend more time in direct patient care.

✓
AI as Clinician's Ally
Ochsner Health implemented an AI-based scheduling system for anesthesiologists. Within six months, the time required to generate department schedules dropped from 60–75 hours per month to just 14 hours — freeing hundreds of hours annually for patient care. Vacation day utilization increased, and staff engagement scores climbed from 3.3 to 4.2 on a 5-point scale. The AI did not replace a single physician. It gave physicians their time back.

In workplace safety, AI tools now analyze voice and speech patterns during virtual meetings to detect early signs of burnout, stress, and emotional fatigue — surfacing risks weeks before they become crises. Major employers including Microsoft, Google, and Johnson & Johnson have integrated these tools with reported improvements in both employee satisfaction and productivity. The digital mental health market is projected to grow from $23.6 billion in 2024 to $27.6 billion in 2025, with corporate wellness as the fastest-expanding segment.

In education, AI tutoring systems are providing personalized learning pathways to students in under-resourced schools that could never afford one-on-one instruction. In climate science, AI is accelerating the modeling of energy systems, agricultural yields, and ocean temperature that would take human scientists decades to compute. In disaster response, computer vision systems are analyzing satellite imagery to identify structural damage within hours of earthquakes or floods, directing aid to where it is most needed.

These applications share a common thread: they extend human capability into domains where human supply is insufficient for human demand. They are not replacements. They are multipliers.

The Hard Facts

The honest data is more nuanced than either the doomsayers or the techno-optimists prefer. Goldman Sachs Research estimates that if current AI use cases were expanded across the entire US economy and reduced employment proportionally to efficiency gains, approximately 2.5% of US employment would be at risk of direct displacement. That is significant, but not civilizational collapse. Their more expansive projection — 6-7% displacement if AI is widely adopted across all sectors — comes with the important caveat that new jobs created by the technology are historically expected to absorb that impact, though with a painful and unequal transition period.

The World Economic Forum projects that by 2030, approximately 92 million jobs will be displaced by AI and automation — while 170 million new ones will emerge. But, as WEF researchers note, these are not one-to-one swaps. The new jobs do not appear in the same locations, in the same industries, or for the same people whose jobs disappeared. The gap between where work vanishes and where it reappears is where genuine hardship lives.

💡
The Most Vulnerable Workers
The National Bureau of Economic Research's 2025 occupational analysis found that approximately 3.9% of US workers — 5 to 6 million people — sit at the intersection of high AI exposure and low adaptive capacity. These are workers in routine roles, with limited savings, in labor markets with few alternative opportunities. This is where the genuine hardship concentrates — not in aggregate statistics, but in specific communities where the only employer just automated their job.

The ITIF's December 2025 analysis found that, through 2024, AI's job-creation effects were outpacing its displacement effects — primarily because the AI boom generated substantial employment in data center construction, hardware manufacturing, and AI development itself. The Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas, reviewing wage data through early 2026, found that in jobs with significant AI exposure, wages were not uniformly declining — suggesting that for many workers, AI is currently augmenting rather than replacing their output. This may not hold as models improve. But the apocalypse has not arrived on schedule.

What has arrived is a structural shift in who gets hired into entry-level roles. Entry-level job postings overall have declined approximately 35% since January 2023, according to Revelio Labs data. Goldman Sachs found that unemployment among 20-to-30-year-olds in AI-exposed tech occupations has risen nearly 3 percentage points since the start of 2025. The pipeline is narrowing at exactly the moment when the next generation most needs to enter it.

What Future Do We Want?

Here is where we must stop counting and start thinking philosophically. Because the statistics tell us what is happening. They do not tell us what should happen. That is a human question — and one that AI, no matter how sophisticated, cannot answer for us.

We stand at a genuinely unprecedented inflection point. Throughout human history, technological revolutions have displaced certain types of labor while creating new categories of work that could not have been imagined before the technology existed. Approximately 60% of US workers today are employed in occupations that did not exist in 1940. The industrial revolution produced enormous suffering and then, over decades, dramatically raised living standards. The digital revolution eliminated entire categories of clerical work and spawned industries worth trillions.

But there is a meaningful difference with AI: the speed. Previous technological transitions unfolded over generations, allowing societies — imperfectly, unequally — to adapt through education, migration, and the slow retraining of workforces. The current wave is measured in quarters. A worker whose role is automated this year has not a decade but perhaps eighteen months to acquire new skills before the next wave makes even those skills insufficient. The human adaptation curve is not keeping pace with the technological displacement curve.

The question is not whether machines can do your job. The question is whether we have built a society where a machine doing your job still provides you a life worth living.

— AIMA Editorial, April 2026

And then there is the question of Artificial General Intelligence. AGI — a system capable of performing any intellectual task a human can perform, across any domain — does not yet exist. But the world's leading AI laboratories are openly pursuing it, with timelines ranging from five to thirty years depending on whom you ask. Leading AI scientists including Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio have warned that a poorly aligned AGI could pose existential risks comparable to nuclear war or pandemic. These are not fringe voices. Hinton won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2024 for his foundational work in neural networks. When he says he is frightened, it is worth paying attention.

What kind of world do we want to inhabit alongside increasingly powerful AI systems? A world where the gains of automation flow primarily to capital, concentrating wealth further in an already unequal society? Or a world where productivity gains fund universal education, healthcare, and perhaps — as some economists argue — a partial basic income that acknowledges the changed nature of work? A world where AI is a tool of surveillance and social control, as it already is in some authoritarian states? Or a world where AI is genuinely in service of human flourishing, deployed transparently, with consent, for purposes humans collectively endorse?

These are not technical questions. They are political, moral, and spiritual ones. Technology does not resolve them. People do — through the choices they make as citizens, consumers, employees, and voters.

Building Guardrails

The good news is that the world is not passively watching. Regulatory frameworks are emerging, imperfectly but meaningfully, to constrain the worst applications of AI and create accountability structures for the rest.

The European Union's AI Act is the most comprehensive regulatory framework currently in force. It adopts a risk-based approach: AI systems are classified as unacceptable risk (banned outright), high risk (strict requirements), limited risk (transparency obligations), or minimal risk (largely unregulated). The prohibition on AI systems using manipulative or deceptive techniques took effect in February 2025. Rules governing general-purpose AI models — including large language models — became enforceable in August 2025. High-risk applications face full compliance deadlines in 2027.

🎙️
The EU AI Act Risk Tiers
Unacceptable risk: Real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring systems, manipulative subliminal techniques — banned. High risk: AI in critical infrastructure, education, employment, law enforcement, border control — strict transparency and human oversight required. Limited risk: Chatbots and generative AI — must disclose AI nature to users. Minimal risk: Spam filters, AI-generated art — largely unregulated. The framework is not perfect, but it is the most serious democratic attempt yet to govern AI at scale.

Beyond regulation, there are practices that individuals, companies, and institutions can implement now — without waiting for legislation — to safeguard against AI malpractice and misuse.

What Companies Can Do Now

Commit to human-in-the-loop requirements for high-stakes decisions: hiring, firing, credit, healthcare, and criminal justice should never be determined by an algorithm alone, without meaningful human review. Establish internal AI ethics boards with genuine authority — not just advisory panels that can be ignored when they become inconvenient. Conduct algorithmic audits for bias before deployment, particularly in hiring and lending tools where discriminatory outcomes are well-documented. Be transparent with employees about how AI is being used in their workplace and what decisions it is influencing. Share the productivity gains: if AI doubles your team's output, invest meaningfully in the people who made that possible, rather than extracting the surplus as pure profit.

What Policymakers Can Do Now

Fund retraining programs at the scale the problem demands. The United States currently spends less than 0.1% of GDP on active labor market policies — one of the lowest rates in the developed world, and woefully insufficient for the pace of change underway. Establish liability frameworks: when an AI system causes harm — in a medical diagnosis, a loan denial, a hiring decision — there must be a clear legal answer to the question "who is responsible?" Mandate algorithmic transparency in high-stakes domains. Invest in AI safety research as seriously as we invest in AI capability research. And create international coordination mechanisms: AI systems built in one country are immediately deployed globally, making purely national regulation only partially effective.

What Individuals Can Do Now

Learn to work alongside AI systems rather than in opposition to them — not because the technology is inevitably right, but because fluency provides agency. Understand how the AI systems that affect your life actually work, well enough to advocate for yourself when they produce unfair outcomes. Support organizations — unions, civil society groups, legislative advocates — that are working to ensure AI governance serves broad public interests rather than narrow ones. And maintain genuine human skills that AI cannot replicate: empathy, moral reasoning, contextual judgment, relational trust. These are not soft skills. They are the core of what makes human beings irreplaceable in each other's lives.

The Unplug Question

Here is a thought experiment worth sitting with. AI cannot function without electricity. Every inference, every model training run, every chatbot response depends on an unbroken chain of power generation, transmission infrastructure, data center cooling, and hardware manufacturing. The AI boom is already straining electrical grids; data centers are projected to consume 8% of US electricity by 2030. The systems we are debating deploying globally require a continuous, massive, and growing energy supply to operate.

Now consider the inverse question: can humans exist without electricity? Technically, yes — for most of human history, we did. But modern western civilization — its food supply chains, water treatment, medical infrastructure, communication networks, financial systems — is now so thoroughly dependent on continuous electrical power that a sustained grid failure at scale would be catastrophic. We have built a civilization that cannot function without the same resource AI requires to operate.

This shared dependency is not a warning against technology. It is a reminder of something profound: we are already so deeply embedded in our technological infrastructure that the question of whether to "use" AI is somewhat illusory. We are not choosing between a technological future and a non-technological one. We are choosing what kind of technological future to build. The electricity flows either way. What changes is who benefits from the systems it powers.

The most urgent AI governance question is therefore not "should we have AI?" — that question is already answered by economic and competitive reality. The question is: on whose terms, toward whose ends, under whose oversight, and with what protections for those who bear the costs?

For AI to genuinely serve humanity, several things must be true simultaneously. The benefits of productivity gains must be distributed broadly, not captured exclusively by capital. Workers displaced by automation must receive meaningful support for transition, not just rhetoric about "jobs of the future." High-stakes AI systems must be auditable, explainable, and contestable by the people they affect. The development of increasingly powerful systems must be matched by increasingly serious safety research. And the voices of communities most affected by AI — workers, patients, students, marginalized groups — must be centered in governance conversations that today are dominated by technologists and investors.

None of this is utopian. It requires no perfect technology, no benevolent corporation, no enlightened government. It requires only the honest acknowledgment that AI is not a natural phenomenon happening to us. It is a set of human choices, made by people with names and intentions and accountability — choices that can be made differently, and better, if enough people insist on it.

🎙️
The Choice Ahead
AI can diagnose disease earlier, extend mental health access to the underserved, accelerate climate solutions, educate children who have no teachers, and free workers from the most dehumanizing forms of labor. It can also concentrate wealth, surveil populations, eliminate livelihoods without replacement, and — in its most advanced future forms — pose risks we cannot yet fully model. The technology does not determine which of these futures arrives. We do. And we are choosing, right now, by what we build, what we permit, what we fund, and what we refuse.

Glossary
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The simulation of human cognitive functions — reasoning, learning, problem-solving, pattern recognition — by computer systems. Modern AI ranges from narrow, task-specific systems (image classifiers, language translators) to large foundation models capable of generalized language and reasoning tasks across many domains.
↑ Back to article
AI Agents
Autonomous AI systems that can perceive their environment, make decisions, and take sequences of actions to achieve defined goals — without requiring a human to direct each individual step. In enterprise contexts, AI agents are increasingly used to handle workflows that once required human employees, such as HR triage, customer service escalation, and data processing.
↑ Back to article
Large Language Model (LLM)
A type of AI system trained on massive datasets of text to predict and generate human-like language. LLMs such as GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini can write, summarize, translate, code, and reason across a wide range of tasks. They are the foundation of most current generative AI products.
↑ Back to article
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
A hypothetical AI system capable of performing any intellectual task that a human being can perform, with comparable flexibility and generality — not just within a specific domain. AGI does not currently exist. Its development timelines are contested among researchers, ranging from within a decade to potentially never. Its potential risks and benefits are the subject of serious scientific and philosophical debate.
↑ Back to article
General-Purpose AI (GPAI)
Under the EU AI Act, general-purpose AI refers to AI models — including large language models — that can perform a wide range of tasks and serve as the foundation for many downstream applications. GPAI models became subject to specific EU regulatory requirements in August 2025, including transparency obligations and, for the most capable models, systemic risk assessment and mitigation duties.
↑ Back to article
Computer Vision
A branch of AI concerned with enabling machines to interpret and understand visual information from the world — images, video, satellite data. Computer vision applications include medical imaging diagnostics, autonomous vehicle perception, satellite damage assessment for disaster response, facial recognition, and industrial quality control.
↑ Back to article
Algorithmic Bias
Systematic and repeatable errors or unfair outcomes in an AI system's outputs that reflect and often amplify biases present in training data, system design, or the way outputs are applied. Algorithmic bias has been documented in hiring tools, facial recognition systems, credit scoring, and predictive policing — often producing worse outcomes for women, people of color, and lower-income individuals.
Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)
A design principle for AI systems that requires meaningful human review or intervention at critical decision points — particularly for high-stakes outcomes such as hiring, medical diagnosis, loan approval, or criminal justice. HITL requirements are a central recommendation in responsible AI governance frameworks and are mandated for high-risk AI applications under the EU AI Act.
